\begin{abstract}
In macro and close-up photos, shallow DoF means that any given shot won't be able to capture the subject in full focus. Therefore, focal-stack fusion overcomes this by merging a series of images taken at varying focus points to create a single image in focus, from the front to the back. This work is based on a systematic study of focus-measures by Pertuz et al.~\cite{pertuz2013analysis} which compared the performance of more than 30 focus-measure operators and showed that the best focus-measure operators to use in shape from focus tasks are gradient-energy measures, especially Tenengrad. This work: Expands on the above methodology by implementing and evaluating the entire focal-stack pipeline (image loading, two-stage ORB-homography and ECC image alignment, focus measure computation, focus map construction, and multi-scale fusion) on six increasingly realistic datasets, a 23-frame plants baseline, a personal 6-frame flower macro stack, a personal 6-frame handheld stack, a 4-frame evening stack, a 3-frame field-captured insect stack, and pre-registered Lytro two-focus pairs. Four focus measures (Laplacian variance, Tenengrad energy, local contrast, and Sum-Modified Laplacian) are systematically compared and three fusion strategies (winner-takes-all, soft/softmax blending, and Laplacian pyramid fusion) are evaluated via Tenengrad sharpness, SSIM, Shannon entropy. Tenengrad is consistently on top of all datasets, thus validating the work of Pertuz et al., which showed that WTA fusion attained the best sharpness gain and pyramid blending best preserved structural similarity.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Macro photography is used to capture fine structure in small subjects like insects, flowers, circuit traces, and more at working distances where the effective depth of field (DoF) may be less than a millimeter. In this regime; anything NOT at the focal plane is going to look blurry, no matter what aperture is used. There are specialized techniques that enable the photographer to manipulate multiple exposures into an all-in-focus image without actually changing the optical system, including focus-bracketed shooting, and computational fusion.

This project is motivated by a motivating scenario, namely macro insect photography using a smartphone camera (Apple iPhone 17 Pro Max in ProRAW mode). The main lens has a fixed aperture of $f/1.78$ with a focal length of 6.765\,mm for very shallow DoF when it is used up close. No macro lenses or extension tubes are needed, rather a series of short photos with refocused depth planes are post processed in software.

This project is directly based on the focus-measure analysis framework of Pertuz et al.~\cite{pertuz2013analysis}. This work is based on their comparative study of the various focus operators Laplacian, Tenengrad, local contrast, and SML, and their conclusion that Tenengrad (Sobel gradient energy) is the most stable operator under different conditions. The project reimplements their comparison on two different datasets, adds multi-scale Laplacian pyramid fusion on top of their pipeline and adds a two-stage ORB+ECC alignment strategy for handheld smartphone stacks, and verifies the results on personal macro photography datasets.

This report makes the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Complete end to end pipeline in python (NumPy, OpenCV, SciPy) without the use of any deep learning framework for the main method.
  \item A systematic comparison of four focus measures applied to two independent datasets (plants and Lytro) which shows that the method ranking is consistent across domains.
  \item A two-stage alignment strategy with ORB feature matching + homography and fine-tuned by ECC that is robust against ghosting caused by focusing breathing (the scale change induced by the refocusing of the lens using the translation-only ECC).
  \item Pipelining on four personal stacks: flower macro, handheld, evening, and insect, acquired using a smartphone.
  \item As exploratory extension, an optional tiny learned focus-scorer (pure NumPy MLP).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

Focal-stack fusion has received extensive study in the field of computational photography. The basic concept of winner-takes-all (WTA) pixel selection using a local sharpness function was presented by Nayar and Nakagawa~\cite{nayar1994shape}. Krotkov~\cite{krotkov1988focusing} developed Laplacian based focus measures, which were refined by Pertuz et al., who systematically benchmarked more than 30 focus measures~\cite{pertuz2013analysis}. It was also found that the Tenengrad criterion which uses the energy of the Sobel gradient is one of the most noise-robust~\cite{santos1997evaluation}. Subbarao and Tyan~\cite{subbarao1998optimal} investigated these different focus measures for depth-from-focus and concluded that gradient energy based measures generalize better over different textures than Laplacian based measures.

Laplacian pyramid blending for seamless multi-focus fusion was proposed by Burt and Adelson~\cite{burt1983multiresolution}. Zhang and Blum categorise the multi-scale fusion of decompositions, and perform a practical study of performance for digital-camera applications~\cite{zhang1999categorization}. Recently, Liu et al.\ showed that deep convolutional features can be used to replace handcrafted focus measures for multi-focus fusion, and produced sharpened composites on benchmark datasets~\cite{liu2017multifocus}. The Lytro two-focus benchmark dataset used in this work was proposed by Nejati et al.~\cite{nejati2015multi}. To register handheld stacks before fusion, ECC-based image alignment is used~\cite{evangelidis2008parametric} and a comprehensive treatment of image registration and alignment is given in Szeliski~\cite{szeliski2010}.

\subsection{Focus Measures}

Let $G_k$ denote the grayscale version of frame $k$ in the stack. Four focus measures are implemented.

\textbf{Laplacian variance.}
\begin{equation}
  S_k^{\text{Lap}}(p) = \bigl[\nabla^2 G_k(p)\bigr]^2
\end{equation}
The squared Laplacian response captures second-order intensity variation at each pixel. A local average over a $W\!\times\!W$ window gives the spatially smoothed score used in the focus map.

\textbf{Tenengrad (Sobel energy).}
\begin{equation}
  S_k^{\text{Ten}}(p) = \bigl[G_x^k(p)\bigr]^2 + \bigl[G_y^k(p)\bigr]^2
\end{equation}
where $G_x^k$ and $G_y^k$ are the horizontal and vertical Sobel responses of frame $k$. This measure is robust to noise and is the primary method used throughout the project.

\textbf{Local contrast.}
\begin{equation}
  S_k^{\text{LC}}(p) = \bigl[G_k(p) - \mu_k^W(p)\bigr]^2
\end{equation}
where $\mu_k^W$ is a local mean over a $W\!\times\!W$ neighbourhood. This is computationally inexpensive but less discriminative near uniform regions.

\textbf{Sum-Modified Laplacian (SML).}
\begin{equation}
  \text{ML}(p) = |2G(p) - G(p\!-\!\delta) - G(p\!+\!\delta)|
\end{equation}
applied separately in horizontal and vertical directions with step $\delta=2$ and summed~\cite{nayar1994shape,subbarao1998optimal}. The SML avoids cancellation of opposing Laplacian signs that arises when positive and negative second-derivative responses cancel in the standard Laplacian.

All focus score volumes are spatially smoothed by a $17\!\times\!17$ local average and then median-filtered (size 5) to reduce salt-and-pepper artefacts in the focus map.

\subsection{Focus Map Construction}

The focus map $F(p) \in \{0,\ldots,N-1\}$ assigns each pixel to the frame with the highest local focus score:
\begin{equation}
  F(p) = \arg\max_{k}\, S_k(p)
\end{equation}

\subsection{Fusion Strategies}

\textbf{Winner-Takes-All (WTA).}
Each output pixel is taken directly from the frame indicated by the focus map:
\begin{equation}
  I^{\text{WTA}}(p) = I_{F(p)}(p)
\end{equation}
This maximises sharpness but can introduce sharp transition artefacts at focus-map boundaries.

\textbf{Soft (softmax) fusion.}
Per-frame weights are computed via a temperature-scaled softmax over the focus scores:
\begin{equation}
  w_k(p) = \frac{\exp\!\bigl(\beta\,\tilde{S}_k(p)\bigr)}{\sum_j \exp\!\bigl(\beta\,\tilde{S}_j(p)\bigr)}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{S}_k = S_k - \max_j S_j$ (for numerical stability) and $\beta=8$. The fused image is $I^{\text{Soft}} = \sum_k w_k \odot I_k$. This produces smooth spatial transitions at the cost of slightly reduced peak sharpness.

\textbf{Laplacian pyramid blending.}
For each frame $k$ and its softmax weight map $w_k$, Laplacian pyramids of the frame and Gaussian pyramids of the weight are generated up to levels $L=5$. The pyramids are combined levelwise and collapsed into the combined output ~\cite{burt1983multiresolution,zhang1999categorization}. This approach not only maintains sharpness, but also continuity in structure.
\subsection{Alignment}

In handheld stacks, camera movement between exposures will cause misregistration creating ghost images in the combined image. This first implementation, with a translation-only ECC~\cite{evangelidis2008parametric}, was found to be inadequate because the lenses of smartphones have a phenomenon called focus breathing, in which the effective focal length varies slightly during the process of refocus, giving rise to a per-frame scale factor of up to 9% between the minimum and maximum focus positions. Translation-only ECC fails to recover scale and when the displacement is large, it falls into wrong local minimas.

To this end, a two-stage alignment approach was thus adopted. Stage~1: Key points of ORB~\cite{rublee2011orb} are extracted from every frame and matched with the reference frame with $k$-NN matcher and Lowe's ratio test ($r=0.75$). The inlier correspondences are used to estimate a homography using RANSAC (with a reprojection threshold of 3\,px). A sanity check prevents degenerate solutions (such as implied rotation > $30^\circ$ or scale factor outside the range $[0.80,\,1.25]$) and in these cases, a phase-correlation estimate initialised ECC translation is used. Stage~2 uses ECC with 100 iterations and a threshold of $10^{-5}$, to refine the sub-pixel residual translation on top of the Stage~1 warp. These ghosting artefacts were not present in the flower, insect, and evening stacks when using translation-only alignment, but they have been removed using this pipeline.

\subsection{Quality Metrics}

Three metrics evaluate fusion quality:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Tenengrad sharpness}: global mean Sobel energy of the fused image (higher is sharper) \cite{santos1997evaluation}.
 \item \textbf{SSIM}~\cite{wang2004ssim}: structural similarity with the reference frame (sharpest single frame); high value indicates that there are more preserved structural contents. Joint measure of the index of luminance, contrast and structure.
 \item \textbf{Shannon entropy}~\cite{shannon1948}: $H = -\sum_i p_i \log_2 p_i$ of the grayscale histogram; higher means a richer intensity distribution and more textural detail.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Personal handheld stack.} Six hand-held images of an indoor scene shot with the iPhone 17 Pro Max. The stack is a scene for testing the pipeline with some standard depth variation. ORB + ECC alignment is used.

\textbf{Evening stack.} Four frames of ProRAW shot during an evening indoor photo session (using the same camera). IMG\_6887 and IMG\_6889 had EXIF ``Rotate 90 CW'' tags and needed to be rotated on the pixel level. IMG\_6888 had a different native width (2900\,px vs.\ 3024\,px) which was centre-cropped to the smallest common width before stacking. ECC alignment is used.

\textbf{Lytro benchmark.} Three pre-registered two-focus pairs from Nejati et al.~\cite{nejati2015multi}: lytro-03 (child and toy), lytro-11 (camera and globe), lytro-13 (zoo sign and tigers). No alignment is applied --- these are an independent cross-domain validation of the focus measure selected.


\subsection{Implementation}

\subsection{Implementation}

The code is all in Python 3 and uses NumPy, OpenCV, SciPy, and Matplotlib and is executed in a Jupyter notebook without the use of any deep learning framework. Images are imported as float32 in [0,1] using PIL and ImageOps.exif\_transpose (to make sure that EXIF rotation tags created by the iPhone camera are applied before any processing step).

The alignment module is based on feature detection with OpenCV's \texttt{ORB\_create}(3000 keypoints), feature matching with a brute-force Hamming-distance matcher and Lowe's ratio test ($r=0.75$), and robust alignment estimation with \texttt{findHomography} and RANSAC. Sub-pixel refinement is done using the \texttt{findTransformECC} function in the translation mode (100 iterations, threshold $10^{-5}$). The focus scores are calculated using \texttt{Sobel} filters and smoothed using \texttt{filter2D} and then post processed using scipy.ndimage.median\_filter for the focus map. The construction and collapsing of Laplacian pyramid is done by OpenCV's pyrDown and pyrUp functions. We used a custom $11\times11$ Gaussian-windowed version of SSIM that is consistent with the formulation of Wang et al.~\cite{wang2004ssim} and Shannon entropy was calculated from a 256-bin grayscale histogram. To allow for manageable memory usage, large images (iPhone ProRAW up to 5712$\times$4284\,px) are downscaled to a maximum long edge of 1920\,px and processed. All fused outputs are stored in the \texttt{outputs/} folder as PNGs.%------------------------------------------------------------

\section{Results and Discussion}
The experimental results on all six data sets are shown in this section, divided into three subsections: selection of a focus measure; comparison of fusion strategies; and per-dataset analysis. For all results, the same fixed hyperparameters as used in Section~\ref{sec:methods} are used: Tenengrad scoring with $17\times17$ averaging window, $5\times5$ median filter on the focus map, and soft and pyramid fusion using softmax temperature $\beta=8$. Reported metrics include Tenengrad sharpness (gradient energy, the higher the better) and SSIM relative to the sharpest single frame (higher means better structural fidelity) as well as Shannon entropy (higher means richer intensity distribution). The best single frame from each experiment is also used as a no-fusion baseline to provide a measure of the gain that can be achieved by fusion alone.

\textbf{SSIM vs.\ sharpness trade-off.} Although WTA always produces the highest Tenengrad score, spatial discontinuities are created at the boundaries of focus-maps due to hard pixel switching, which results in the lowest SSIM. The difference between soft and pyramid blending comes down to the compromise of some loss of peak sharpness, but smoother transitions, and higher fidelity in structure.

\textbf{Pyramid vs.\ soft.} For most images, the results of the two methods are almost identical (e.g., Lytro-13: 0.872 vs.\ 0.871). This marginal difference is in line with theory as pyramid blending explicitly decorrelates artefacts between frequency bands, but on low-resolution stacks ($520\!\times\!520$), the gain is limited.

\section{Optional Extension: Tiny Learned Focus-Scorer}

For exploratory purposes, a lightweight MLP was trained on pseudo-labels obtained from the classical Tenengrad focus map. Table~\ref{tab:mlp_config} summarises the network configuration.

\begin{table}[!ht]
\centering
\caption{Tiny MLP configuration.}
\label{tab:mlp_config}
\resizebox{\columnwidth}{!}{%
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
Hyperparameter & Value \\
\midrule
Input features     & 4 (Tenengrad, Laplacian, Contrast, SML; normalised) \\
Hidden layers      & $10 + 6$ units, \texttt{tanh} activation \\
Output             & Softmax over $N$ frames \\
Pseudo-labels      & Classical Tenengrad focus map \\
Training epochs    & 140 \\
Validation accuracy& $\sim$81\% \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}%
}
\end{table}

A 4-element feature vector per frame (Tenengrad, Laplacian, local contrast, min-max normalised SML scores) is fed into the network and a frame probability vector is returned. The network is written completely in NumPy, no deep-learning framework is needed. The main advantage is that the classical focus map can be reproduced through only a simple learned module, without to handcraft any focus rules, which suggests that the method can be naturally extended to situations where the classical method fails (such as diffuse textures and chromatic defocus)~\cite{liu2017multifocus}. The same self-supervised MLP approach was also tested on the evening stack; it also trained at a similar rate and results in a similar focus map as the classical approach, showing that the training approach is not specific to the plants dataset and generalises across scene types.

\section{Conclusion}

This project proves that a totally classical approach to all-in-focus composite (Tenengrad focus scoring, focus-map smoothing and multi-scale fusion) is reliable throughout datasets from plants shot on a tripod to close-up and macro shots taken with a smartphone held by hand. The main conclusions are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Among the four tested focus measures, Tenengrad (Sobel gradient energy) performs best and its position in comparison to the other measures is consistent in both independent data sets (plants and Lytro).
  \item Winner-takes-all fusion maximises sharpness (up to $+$233\% over the best single frame for the flower stack), but degrades SSIM; Laplacian pyramid blending provides the best structural-similarity--sharpness trade-off.
  \item A two-stage ORB-homography + ECC alignment pipeline successfully reduces the influence of camera shake and focus breathing (lens scale change) in handheld stacks, while not being able to compensate for the influence of subject motion (pyramid blending is more robust in this case: insect: 0.680 vs. 0.649).
  \item The classical pseudo-labels are used to train a tiny MLP, which reproduces the classical focus map with 81\% pixel accuracy, thus proving the feasibility of a learned focus-scorer as a future extension.
\end{enumerate}

Future work may involve further stages in the pipeline, such as segmenting the subject through the video, aligning the optical flow of the moving subjects, or learning-based focus measures trained on real world macro datasets, where depth is annotated.
